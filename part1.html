An introductory guide to Computational Thinking

To my understanding, computational thinking is a logical way of understanding and solving problems based on computer science related basic concepts and related components. Computational thinking includes decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, algorithm, and generalisation. Decomposition and pattern recognition divide complex problems or tasks into simple manageable sub-problems or sub-tasks. Abstraction is to remove information that has nothing to do with the solution of the problem and retain relevant information. Algorithms are specific logic and steps to solve problems. Generalisation is the application of algorithms to new problems.

Consider the following example, i.e., we want to provide tea to a group of guests. First, we break down the task of making a cup of tea into a series of steps: boiling water, putting tea bags, making tea, filtering tea, and pouring tea. In this process, we identified some patterns: such as the water temperature range, and the time to make tea. We have removed some irrelevant information through abstraction, for example, using what kind of teacup and kettle. We perform specific steps, which is equivalent to implementing specific algorithms. Finally, we can use the same method to make many cups of tea to serve multiple people, which corresponds to generalisation.

In general, computational thinking is about how to think in a computational way to simplify and solve problems, and our future courses will realise this way of thinking by designing algorithms and writing programmes. For example, in the future, we will learn some commonly used algorithms and data structures to study how to solve specific problems efficiently. Designing algorithms to solve problems requires us to have a logical way of thinking, which is the computational thinking in this module. Therefore, computational thinking is important for learning more advanced courses in my study of computer science programme.
